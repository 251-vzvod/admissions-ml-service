# InVisionU Scoring Principles

This document translates the InVision University brief into practical scoring principles for the ML/NLP screening service.

It is not a generic admissions rubric.

It reflects what the service should optimize for if it is meant to support InVisionU specifically.

## Core Interpretation

The system should not try to identify the most polished applicants.

It should help the committee surface applicants who are more likely to become:

- values-based leaders
- practical problem-solvers
- community-oriented builders
- early-stage founders and changemakers

This means the service should reward:

- action on real problems
- growth through friction
- practical initiative
- responsibility toward others
- willingness to build, test, and improve

It should reward these even when:

- the writing is not highly polished
- the candidate comes from a constrained context
- the evidence is still early-stage rather than elite

## What Counts As A Strong Signal

The following are positive signals for InVisionU-style selection:

- Candidate noticed a concrete problem in a school, neighborhood, team, or community setting.
- Candidate did something real, even if small, instead of only describing concern.
- Candidate can explain what changed after their action.
- Candidate shows reflection after failure, friction, or incomplete success.
- Candidate appears motivated by usefulness, contribution, or social impact rather than prestige alone.
- Candidate shows signs of project-based thinking:
  - building
  - testing
  - iterating
  - improving
- Candidate appears able to work across people, roles, or practical constraints.
- Candidate demonstrates leadership through responsibility and follow-through, not only through titles.

## What Counts As A Weak Signal

The following should not be treated as strong evidence on their own:

- polished English
- abstract leadership language
- prestige-coded self-presentation
- broad future ambition without concrete prior action
- long responses that contain little grounded evidence
- confident tone without examples, obstacles, or outcomes

These may improve readability, but they should not substitute for actual evidence of initiative, growth, and contribution.

## What Must Not Drive Merit

The following must not be used as merit advantages:

- family wealth or hardship as such
- school prestige
- geography by itself
- social class proxies
- protected characteristics
- essay polish by itself
- access to expensive extracurricular opportunities

These may be relevant for support planning or manual context review, but not as direct merit rewards.

## Signals That Matter Most For InVisionU

### 1. Leadership Through Action

The service should prefer:

- concrete initiative
- responsibility taken without being forced
- coordination, follow-through, and usefulness

over:

- formal titles without substance
- generic leadership rhetoric

### 2. Trajectory Of Growth

The service should prefer candidates who show:

- challenge
- adaptation
- reflection
- improvement

This is especially important because InVisionU is not selecting only for current polish. It is selecting for future builders and leaders.

### 3. Community Orientation

The service should reward signals that the candidate:

- notices real social or local problems
- cares about improving life around them
- connects learning with contribution

This should matter more than purely self-focused achievement narratives.

### 4. Project-Based Readiness

The service should reward:

- building something small but real
- solving repeated practical problems
- testing an idea in the real world
- learning from imperfect implementation

This is highly aligned with InVisionU's project-based model.

### 5. Promising But Needs Support

This is not a failure category.

For InVisionU, it is often a strategically valuable group:

- candidates with real underlying signal
- candidates with weaker current polish
- candidates who may need language, academic, or onboarding support

The system should surface this cohort clearly rather than burying it.

## Candidate Types The System Should Surface

The service should actively try to surface:

- hidden-potential candidates from constrained contexts
- early-stage doers
- community-oriented builders
- candidates with credible growth and reflection
- promising candidates who are not yet polished but could thrive with support

## Candidate Types The System Should Treat Carefully

The service should be careful not to overvalue:

- polished-but-thin applicants
- applicants whose narratives are broad but weakly evidenced
- applicants who sound impressive but show little practical initiative
- applicants whose values language is strong but whose real-world action is unclear

## Product Implication

The product should communicate something like this:

We do not help the committee reward the best self-presentation.

We help the committee identify future leaders who already show practical initiative, growth, and value-driven action, even when their application is not the most polished.

## Scoring Implication

In practice, this means the service should bias toward:

- evidence of action over rhetoric
- growth over polish
- grounded motivation over abstract inspiration
- contribution over prestige signaling
- support-aware potential over narrow elite filtering

## Final Rule

Any future scoring change should pass this question:

Would this change make the system better at surfacing practical, values-based, growth-oriented future leaders for InVisionU?

If not, it is probably not the right priority.
